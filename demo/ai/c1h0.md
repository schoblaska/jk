## The Partnership
tags: #history

Noah Cross and Hollis Mulwray co-founded the private Los Angeles Water Company in 1914, combining Cross's capital with Mulwray's engineering expertise. Together they built the infrastructure that brought Owens Valley water to Los Angeles - the project that made the city's growth possible.

For a decade they were the most powerful partnership in Southern California. Cross handled the business and the politics. Mulwray handled the water.

## The Split
tags: #water

By 1923, Mulwray had grown uncomfortable with Cross's approach to the water business - specifically, Cross's interest in keeping the supply private and using control of water access as leverage over land development. Mulwray believed water was a public resource. Cross believed it was a commodity.

Mulwray went to the city council and advocated for public ownership of the water supply. The council agreed. In 1924, the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power absorbed the private water company. Mulwray became Chief Engineer. Cross was bought out.

## The Fallout

- Cross lost control of the most valuable resource in the region
- He reportedly told associates that Mulwray had "stolen" the water from him
- The personal rift deepened when [Evelyn Cross](../e1c0) married Mulwray shortly after the public takeover - Cross saw it as a betrayal on two fronts
- Cross and Mulwray did not speak publicly after 1925
- Cross retained his fortune through real estate and other holdings, but colleagues say the loss of the water supply never left him
