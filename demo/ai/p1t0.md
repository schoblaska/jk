## Summary

A review of LA County property transfer records for the northwest San Fernando Valley (January–June 1937) reveals an unusual concentration of purchases - 47 parcels totaling approximately 8,200 acres of agricultural land.

## Buyers
tags: #shell-companies

The parcels were acquired through a variety of entities with no apparent connection to one another:

- **Emma Dill Trust** - 6 parcels, 940 acres
- **Jasper Lamar Crabb Estate** - 4 parcels, 720 acres
- **Pacific Shores Retirement Home** - 5 parcels, 810 acres
- **First Church of Christ, Scientist (Glendale)** - 3 parcels, 480 acres
- **Mae Barsotti** (age 83, listed as "homemaker") - 4 parcels, 690 acres
- **Roaring Fork Land Co.** - 7 parcels, 1,100 acres
- Various other individuals and trusts - 18 parcels, 3,460 acres

## Pattern
tags: #evidence

- All purchases occurred within a 6-month window
- Prices paid are 30–50% below assessed value - consistent with distressed sales
- Sellers are almost exclusively small-scale farmers and ranchers
- Buyer entities share no common officers, addresses, or legal representation on paper
- Several "individual" buyers are elderly (ages 78–91), an unusual demographic for large land speculation

## Red Flags

- The diversity of buyer names combined with the uniformity of timing and geography is the hallmark of a coordinated purchasing operation using fronts
- Retirement homes and churches do not typically speculate in agricultural land
- The depressed prices coincide exactly with the onset of the [drought](../d1r0) and reported well failures

## Assessment

This is not independent purchasing activity. Someone with significant capital is assembling a large land position in the valley through proxies designed to obscure ownership. The connection to the [drought timeline](../d1r0) and the [Alto Vallejo Dam](../a1v0) proposal is hard to ignore: if the dam is built, water flows to these parcels, and their value multiplies. Identifying the actual beneficiary behind these shell buyers is the key.
